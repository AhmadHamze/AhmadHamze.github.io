I consider myself to be someone who uses logic and computers to solve problems. Whether it's about creating an AI from scratch or simply explaining a math problem to a student, it's always about solving problems.

After I got a master's degree in applied mathematics from the University of Bordeaux in France, I wasn't ready to commit myself for three more years getting a Ph.D. in this field. I wanted to do something more practical, moreover, I felt that I don't belong to academia.
I was always fascinated by computers, especially how they do mathematics. How do they draw complex graphs so quickly?
How is it possible for a computer to simulate random numbers?
I remember applying the Monte-Carlo simulation to estimate 𝛑; I was thrilled by how easy it is to test such an advanced method on computers.

I wanted to know more about the world of programming, not only its uses for math but in general.
I wanted to start by learning the basics, so I chose C++ as my first language to learn, even though I was discouraged to do so. I wanted to know what memory management looks like and what are pointers.
One thing led to another, and here I am, completely drawn into this world, learning new concepts every single day.
